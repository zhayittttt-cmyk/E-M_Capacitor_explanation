Charging a Capacitor

A rigorous explanation using potentials, not electric fields

The Setup

Consider connecting two uncharged conducting plates (a parallel plate capacitor) to a battery of voltage \(V\). Let's fix the ground at the midpoint of the battery, so the positive terminal is at \(+V/2\) and the negative terminal is at \(-V/2\).

The Key Observations

Observation 1: A battery is not a conductor. Its terminal voltages are fixed by its internal electrochemical properties. The battery is the boss — it dictates the potentials at its terminals.

Observation 2: The wire connecting a terminal to a plate, together with the plate itself, forms a single conductor. From electrostatics (end of Chapter 2), we know that a conductor maintains electrostatic equilibrium: \(\vec{E} = 0\) inside, and the potential is constant throughout.

This gives us two sets of conductors. The left set (positive terminal + wire + left plate) is at \(+V/2\), and the right set (negative terminal + wire + right plate) is at \(-V/2\).

Finding the Charge

Now that we know the potential on each plate, we ask: how much charge is needed to make this configuration self-consistent?

Between the plates, we still have a parallel plate capacitor. The electric field in the gap is related to the surface charge density by Gauss's law, and related to the potential difference by \(V = Ed\). Combining these:

$$Q = A\,\varepsilon_0\,E = A\,\varepsilon_0\,\frac{V}{d}$$

The coefficient of \(V\) here, namely \(\dfrac{A\,\varepsilon_0}{d}\), is what we call the capacitance \(C\) of the capacitor — a concept you will see formally in the next chapter. So we arrive at:

$$Q = CV$$

Why Does Current Stop?

Eventually, when the voltages are balanced — each plate matching the potential of its connected terminal — the charges have no potential difference driving them, so they have nowhere to move. Current stops, and the system is in equilibrium.

The Bigger Lesson: Forget \(\vec{E}\), Think \(V\)

In circuits, the shape of wires can be arbitrary and irregular. We have never seen how to quantify the electric field inside an irregular conductor — that would require multivariable calculus and is extremely difficult in general. What we can work with is potential.

We proved, using electric fields, that conductors maintain electrostatic equilibrium (constant potential throughout). Having established this fact, we can now set the electric field reasoning aside and work entirely with potentials. This is the right way to think about circuits.

Analogy

This is similar to the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. We use Part 1 to prove Part 2, but then we never use Part 1 again to compute definite integrals — Part 2 is the operational tool. Similarly, the electric field derivation of electrostatic equilibrium is the foundational justification, but potential is the tool you actually compute with in circuits.

You will see this idea of working with potential (rather than electric fields) come to full fruition when you reach the chapter on Kirchhoff's Rules.
