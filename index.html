Naively Charging a Capacitor

A rigorous explanation using potentials, not electric fields

The Intuitive Picture

It is most natural to think of the uncharged plates as starting at \(0\,\text{V}\), with the positive terminal of the battery at \(+V/2\) and the negative terminal at \(-V/2\). You can then visualize charges "rolling downhill" from the terminals toward the plates. This picture is useful for building intuition, but it is only for intuition.

Why We Should Think in Terms of Potential

When dealing with circuits, the electric field is not really the right thing to think about, because the shape of a circuit is never trivial — the wires can be bent into any shape you want. What we can work with is potential. At the end of Chapter 2, we derived the fact that a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium satisfies: \(\vec{E} = 0\) inside, and \(V = \text{constant}\) throughout.

This is really the only tool we have for dealing with irregularly shaped conductors, because we have never seen how to quantify the electric field inside an irregular shape — doing so would require multivariable calculus. So we would rather think about the charging process in terms of potential instead. The explanation using electric fields is only there to help you make sense of things, because that is what you are familiar with from earlier chapters. But once you get used to circuits, what you should always be thinking about is potential. I am not saying electric fields are not important; they are absolutely crucial for you to understand what happens within a capacitor. Yet when it comes to analysis of circuits, potential is the better one to work with.

The Mathematically Coherent Way

Key fact: A battery is not a conductor. Its terminal potentials are fixed by its internal electrochemical properties.

The wire together with the plate on each side forms a conductor. So we have two sets of conductors: the left set (positive terminal + wire + left plate) and the right set (negative terminal + wire + right plate).

Since each set is a conductor, it tends toward electrostatic equilibrium — meaning that once the charging process is finished, the potential is constant throughout each set. During the brief charging process, there can be small electric fields in the wires that allow current to flow. In the final steady state, the left set settles to the potential of the positive terminal (\(+V/2\) in our example), and the right set settles to the potential of the negative terminal (\(-V/2\) in our example).

Note on the choice of ground: It does not actually matter whether we label the terminals as \(+7.5\,\text{V}\) and \(-7.5\,\text{V}\), or \(15\,\text{V}\) and \(0\,\text{V}\), or \(0\,\text{V}\) and \(-15\,\text{V}\), or any other pair. The point is that each plate will match the potential of the terminal it is connected to in the final equilibrium state. Only the difference in potential matters physically.

Finding the Charge

Now, to figure out how much charge the plates have: remember that we still have a parallel plate capacitor, where the electric field between the plates is related to the surface charge density. (Electric fields are still important!) The potential difference across the plates is related to the field by \(V = Ed\). Combining these gives:

$$Q = A\,\varepsilon_0\,E = A\,\varepsilon_0\,\frac{V}{d}$$

In fact, the coefficient of \(V\) here — namely \(\dfrac{A\,\varepsilon_0}{d}\) — is what we call the capacitance \(C\) of the capacitor. This formula assumes the plates are large compared to their separation so that edge effects are small and the electric field between them is approximately uniform.

Why Does Current Stop?

Eventually, when the capacitor’s voltage equals the battery’s voltage, there is no net potential difference driving charges through the circuit. The potentials inside the two conductors become constants, thus the charges will no longer move. (The electric field inside the wires becomes zero, so current stops. )

The Bigger Lesson

So really, we prove the fact that conductors remain in electrostatic equilibrium once, using electric fields (though we did not do the proof rigorously, which is the hard part), and then we set that reasoning aside because it is not easy to work with. From that point on, we use the consequence — that potential is constant throughout a conductor in equilibrium — as our working tool.

Analogy

A familiar (yet perhaps unpleasant) analogy: we used the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus Part 1 to prove Part 2, yet we would never use Part 1 to compute any definite integrals. Similarly, the electric field derivation justifies the equilibrium principle, but potential is the tool you actually compute with in circuits.

You will see this idea of working with potential rather than electric fields come to full use when you reach the chapter on Kirchhoff's Rules.
